# Scam Alerts — April 13, 2026

## AI Voice and Video Scams Are Getting Harder to Spot

Scammers are now using artificial intelligence to create fake phone calls that sound exactly like someone you trust — a family member, a bank employee, or even a government official. They can clone a voice using just a few seconds of audio found online, and they can make videos of celebrities or professionals that look completely real. These fakes are being used to push you toward sending money, sharing passwords, or clicking suspicious links.

The old warning signs no longer apply. These messages no longer have obvious spelling mistakes or awkward phrasing — they look and sound polished and professional. Instead, watch for urgency ("you must act now"), unusual payment requests like gift cards or cryptocurrency, or any unexpected call asking you to verify personal information.

If something feels off, hang up and call the person or company back using a number you look up yourself — never the one they gave you. You can also create a family "safe word" that only you and your loved ones know, so you can verify it really is them in an emergency. Report suspicious calls to the FBI at IC3.gov or to AARP's Fraud Watch helpline: 877-908-3360.

## Tech Support Scams Are Showing Up in Retirement Communities

Scammers are specifically targeting older adults in retirement communities with a common trick: a pop-up or phone call warns that your computer has a virus and tells you to call a number immediately. When you call, someone pretending to be from Apple or Microsoft explains that your money is at risk and you need to withdraw cash and deposit it into a special "safe" account — often through a cryptocurrency ATM at a nearby gas station or pharmacy.

This is a scam. No real tech company will call you out of the blue or ask you to move your money to protect it. The IRS, FBI, Medicare, and Social Security Administration will never threaten you by phone or demand immediate payment. If you get one of these calls, hang up.

If you're unsure whether a warning is real, ask a trusted friend or family member before doing anything. You can also call AARP's Fraud Watch helpline (877-908-3360) and speak with a trained volunteer who can help you figure out what to do.

## Criminals Are Stealing Airline Miles and Hotel Points

Your frequent flyer miles, hotel points, and credit card rewards are worth real money — and criminals know it. Thieves use stolen passwords (from old data breaches) to break into loyalty accounts, then quickly cash out your points for gift cards or merchandise before you even notice anything is wrong. Because most people don't check their rewards accounts very often, the theft can go undetected for months.

To protect your points, use a different password for each loyalty account and turn on two-factor authentication (a code texted to your phone when you log in). Set up account alerts so you get a notification any time points are redeemed.

If you notice points missing, contact the airline or hotel rewards program right away — many will work with you to restore stolen points if you catch it quickly. You can also report the theft to the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center at IC3.gov.
