# Scam Alerts — April 6, 2026

## Fake "Unclaimed Money" Calls and Texts

Scammers are calling and texting people to say they have thousands of dollars in unclaimed funds waiting for them — all they need to do is share some personal information or pay a small "processing fee" to collect it. This is a trick. There is no money. The goal is to steal your information or get you to send cash.

Real government agencies do not call you out of the blue asking for personal details or fees to release money. If you want to check whether you genuinely have unclaimed property, you can look it up yourself for free at your state's official unclaimed property website — no phone call needed.

If you get one of these calls or messages, just hang up or ignore it. Do not share your Social Security number, bank account details, or any payment. You can report it at ReportFraud.ftc.gov.

## Fake Product Recall Messages

You may receive an email or text that looks like it came from Amazon, Walmart, Costco, or another familiar store, saying one of your recent purchases has been recalled and you need to click a link to claim your refund. The message looks official, but it is fake. The link is designed to steal your login information or financial details.

Real product recalls do not usually ask you to click a link or sign into your account through a message. Legitimate recalls advise you to stop using the item — they rarely require immediate action through a text or email.

If you get one of these messages, do not click any links. Instead, go directly to the store's website by typing the address yourself, or check cpsc.gov/Recalls for official recall information. If you think you've been targeted, you can call the AARP Fraud Watch Network helpline for free at 877-908-3360.

## Unexpected Calls Offering Mortgage Help

If you've been struggling to pay your mortgage — maybe because of a job change, health bills, or a recent disaster — you might suddenly get a call from someone offering to help. They may sound very professional and claim to work with a government program or loan servicer. But unsolicited calls like this are often scams designed to take your money and leave you in a worse situation.

Legitimate mortgage relief programs do not cold-call you. Real help is available, but you should seek it out yourself by contacting your loan servicer directly using the number on your statement, or by visiting the official Consumer Financial Protection Bureau website at consumerfinance.gov.

Never pay upfront fees to someone who called you out of the blue. Hang up, look up your servicer's real number, and call them yourself if you need help.
