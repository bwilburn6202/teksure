## Post 1 — Trending / Urgent

### Title
**How to Spot a Voice Cloning Scam Call (and What to Do Right Away)**

### Meta Description (158 chars)
Scammers can now copy a loved one's voice in seconds. Here's how to spot a voice cloning scam call, what to ask, and how to keep your family safe.

### Target keyword
voice cloning scam call

### Suggested URL slug
`/blog/voice-cloning-scam-call`

### Estimated length
1,050 words

---

#### Opening (Hook — empathy first)

The phone rings. It's your grandson. He sounds upset, maybe a little out of breath. He says he's been in a small accident, he's okay, but he needs help — and he doesn't want his parents to know yet.

It sounds like him. The pauses, the way he says your name. Everything.

Here's the hard part: it might not be him.

Scammers can now copy someone's voice using just a few seconds of audio — often pulled from a Facebook video or a TikTok. They use that copy to call grandparents and ask for money. The FTC says these calls are now one of the fastest-growing scams in the country.

The good news: there are simple things you can do, in the moment, to protect yourself and your family. You don't need to be a tech expert. You just need to know what to listen for.

---

#### Section 1: How voice cloning scams actually work (in plain English)

A scammer finds a public video of a family member online — a wedding toast, a graduation, a TikTok dance. They feed that short clip into a free AI tool. Within minutes, the tool can make that voice say anything the scammer types.

Then they call you. They use your loved one's "voice" and rush you. The whole point is to get you to act before you think.

That rush is the giveaway.

---

#### Section 2: 5 signs the call is a voice clone

1. **They're in a hurry.** Real emergencies have time for "let me call you back from a different phone." Scammers do not.
2. **They ask for money — gift cards, wire, or cash courier.** No real grandchild needs Apple gift cards to pay a lawyer.
3. **They beg you not to tell other family members.** Isolation is the scammer's tool.
4. **The story has odd gaps.** They might know your name but not the dog's name. Or they say "Grandma" when your grandkids call you "Nana."
5. **The number is unfamiliar or "spoofed."** Even a familiar number can be faked.

---

#### Section 3: The one question to ask (your family safe word)

Pick a word with your family today. Any word. *Pancake. Maple. Buttercup.* It doesn't matter what it is — only that no one outside your family knows it.

When a call feels off, ask: **"What's our family word?"**

A scammer can clone a voice. They cannot guess a word you set up at the kitchen table.

If they hesitate, change the subject, or get angry — hang up.

---

#### Section 4: What to do right now if you got a suspicious call

1. **Hang up.** You don't owe a scammer politeness.
2. **Call your loved one directly** at the number you already have saved. Not the number that just called you.
3. **If you can't reach them**, call another family member. Don't let the scammer's "don't tell anyone" instruction stick.
4. **Report it.** File a quick report at [reportfraud.ftc.gov](https://reportfraud.ftc.gov). It takes about 5 minutes and helps the FTC track these scams.
5. **Tell one friend.** The fastest way to protect your community is to talk about it.

---

#### Section 5: How to lower your risk going forward

- **Lock down social media.** Set Facebook and Instagram videos to "Friends only." Scammers grab voice clips from public posts.
- **Don't answer unknown numbers.** If it's important, they'll leave a voicemail.
- **Set up the family safe word today.** Five minutes, free, works forever.

---

## Post 2 — Anchor / Evergreen

### Title
**What Is AI? A Plain-English Guide for Beginners (No Jargon)**

### Meta Description (159 chars)
Wondering what is AI and why everyone is talking about it? Here's a plain-English guide for beginners — no jargon, no hype, just the basics that matter.

### Target keyword
what is AI

### Suggested URL slug
`/blog/what-is-ai`

### Estimated length

---

#### Opening (Hook — empathy first)

Lately, "AI" is everywhere. It's on the news. It's a button on your phone. Your grandkids are using it for homework. And nobody seems to be stopping to explain what it actually is.

If you've been nodding along when people mention AI, hoping no one asks what you think — this guide is for you.

No jargon. No hype. Just the plain-English version of what AI is, what it does, and what you actually need to know.

---

#### Section 1: AI in one sentence

**AI is a computer program that has read a lot of stuff and learned to guess what comes next.**

That's it. That's the whole idea.

When you type "What's a good chicken soup recipe?" into ChatGPT, it doesn't *know* the answer the way your aunt does. It has read millions of recipes and is making a very good guess about what a chicken soup recipe should look like.

Most of the time, that guess is helpful. Sometimes it's wrong. We'll get to that.

---

#### Section 2: Where you've already met AI

You've been using AI for years without thinking about it:

- **Spam folder in your email** — AI guesses which messages are junk.
- **Suggested replies** in texts ("On my way!") — AI guesses what you'd type next.
- **Photo search** ("show me pictures of the dog") — AI recognized the dog.
- **Maps rerouting around traffic** — AI is predicting traffic patterns.

The new part isn't AI itself. The new part is that AI can now *talk back* in full sentences.

---

#### Section 3: The new tools you keep hearing about

Here's the short version of the names in the news:

- **ChatGPT** — a chatbot from a company called OpenAI. You type a question, it answers in plain English.
- **Google Gemini** — Google's version of the same thing. Often built into Android phones.
- **Apple Intelligence** — Apple's version, built into newer iPhones and iPads.
- **Microsoft Copilot** — Microsoft's version, built into Windows and Word.

They all do roughly the same thing: read what you type, write back in sentences.

You only need to know one of them. Pick the one that comes with the phone or computer you already have.

---

#### Section 4: What AI is good at (and not good at)

**Good at:**

- Writing a polite email when you're stuck on words
- Explaining something in simpler language ("explain this medical letter to me like I'm not a doctor")
- Summarizing long articles
- Brainstorming ("give me 5 birthday card messages for my sister")
- Translation

**Not good at:**

- Anything that needs to be 100% factually correct (it can guess wrong)
- Personal advice (it doesn't know you)
- Anything involving real-time information unless it's connected to the internet
- Replacing your doctor, lawyer, or financial advisor

A good rule: AI is great as a *first draft* helper. Always check anything important.

---

#### Section 5: Is AI safe to use?

Mostly yes — with two simple rules:

1. **Don't put private information into a chatbot.** Don't paste your Social Security number, bank numbers, or full medical records. Treat it like talking to a stranger on a bench.
2. **Don't trust it on anything that matters without checking.** AI sometimes makes things up — confidently. Especially names, dates, and quotes.

If you keep those two rules, you can experiment freely.

---

#### Section 6: Try it once, today

Open ChatGPT (free, at chat.openai.com) and type one question:

> *"Explain what AI is to me like I'm 70 years old and not a tech person."*

You'll have your first AI conversation in under a minute. That's all there is to it.
