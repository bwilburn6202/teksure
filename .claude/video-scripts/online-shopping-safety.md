# Video Script: Online Shopping Safety
**Slug:** online-shopping-safety
**Target length:** 80 seconds
**Format:** Screen recording with voiceover

---

## HOOK (5 seconds)

> That amazing deal might be a fake website built to steal your card details. Here's how to check before you pay a cent.

---

## WALKTHROUGH (65 seconds)

> Every time you shop online, start with this quick check. Look at the top of your browser at the website address. You should see a padlock icon and the address should start with https — that "s" stands for secure. No padlock? Leave immediately.

> Next, look at the website name very carefully. Scammers create sites like "amazon-deals.net" or "walmart.discount-store.com" that look real. The real Amazon is just amazon.com. The real Walmart is just walmart.com. Nothing extra.

> Third check — the prices. If everything on the site is 70 or 80 percent off with no explanation, that's a major warning sign. Real sales exist, but a store selling everything at a fraction of retail is almost always fake.

> Check the payment options. Legitimate stores accept credit cards and PayPal. If a website only takes bank transfers, gift cards, or cryptocurrency — leave. These payment methods have no consumer protection, meaning you can't get your money back.

> When you do shop online, use a credit card if you have one. Credit cards offer the strongest protection. If an item doesn't arrive or isn't as described, your card company has to refund you. Debit cards and PayPal also offer disputes — but a bank transfer is gone for good.

> If a purchase goes wrong, contact the seller first and give them five days to respond. If they ignore you, open a dispute through the platform — Amazon's A-to-Z Guarantee, eBay's Money Back Guarantee, or PayPal's Resolution Center. Paid by credit card? Call your card company and ask for a chargeback.
