# Scam Prevention

## Why Scams Target Older Adults

Scammers target people of all ages, but they frequently go after older adults because they tend to be more trusting and may be less familiar with digital tricks. Knowing the warning signs is your best defense.

## Fraud by the Numbers (2025-2026)

The scale of fraud in the United States continues to grow:

- **$15.9 billion** in total fraud losses were reported in 2025, across more than **3 million reports** filed with the FTC. (Source: FTC.gov, 2026)
- **Imposter scams** — where someone pretends to be a trusted person or organization — were the **#1 reported fraud type**, causing **$3.5 billion in losses** alone. (Source: FTC.gov, 2026)
- Reports from older adults losing **$10,000 or more** to fraud **quadrupled** between 2020 and 2024. (Source: FTC.gov, 2026)
- Key tactics include **fake fraud alerts** (messages claiming your bank detected suspicious activity), **imposter calls using stolen personal data** to sound convincing, and **AI voice clones** that mimic a family member's voice. (Source: FTC.gov, 2026)
- In March 2026, the FTC hosted a roundtable focused specifically on the **top scams affecting older adults**, signaling increased federal attention to this issue. (Source: consumer.ftc.gov, 2026)

## Common Scam Types

- **Tech support scams:** A pop-up or phone call claims your computer has a virus. A real company will never call you out of the blue about this.
- **Phishing emails and texts:** Messages that look like they are from your bank, Amazon, or the IRS, asking you to click a link or share personal information.
- **Grandparent scams:** A caller pretends to be a grandchild in trouble and begs for money. Always hang up and call your family member directly.
- **IRS and SSN scams:** The IRS will never call and threaten arrest. Anyone who does is a scammer.
- **Romance scams:** Someone you meet online builds a relationship, then asks for money. Never send money to someone you have not met in person.
- **Fake online shopping:** Websites with prices that seem too good to be true usually are.
- **Investment scams:** Promises of guaranteed high returns are always a red flag.
- **AI voice clone scams:** Scammers can now copy a loved one's voice using artificial intelligence. If someone calls asking for money, verify by calling them back on a number you already have.

## Universal Red Flags

- Urgency: "Act now or lose your account!"
- Requests for gift cards, wire transfers, or cryptocurrency.
- Unsolicited calls, texts, or emails asking for personal information.
- Spelling mistakes and odd email addresses.
- Threats of arrest, lawsuits, or account suspension.

## What to Do If You Have Been Scammed

1. **Do not feel embarrassed** — scammers are professionals and this happens to millions of people.
2. Contact your bank immediately to stop or reverse charges.
3. Report the scam to the **FTC** at **ReportFraud.ftc.gov**.
4. Report internet crime to the **FBI's IC3** at **ic3.gov**.
5. Call the **AARP Fraud Helpline** at **877-908-3360**.
