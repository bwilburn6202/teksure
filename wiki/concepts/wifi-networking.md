# Wi-Fi and Networking

## What Is Wi-Fi?

Wi-Fi is the wireless signal that lets your phone, tablet, or computer connect to the internet without plugging in a cable. A small box called a **router** (usually provided by your internet company) sends out this signal throughout your home. Think of it like a radio station — your devices "tune in" to get online.

## How to Connect to Wi-Fi

1. Open **Settings** on your device.
2. Tap **Wi-Fi** (or **Network & Internet** on some devices).
3. You will see a list of available networks. Look for your home network name — it is often printed on a sticker on your router.
4. Tap the network name and type in the password (also on that sticker).
5. Once connected, you should see a small fan-shaped icon at the top of your screen.

## Troubleshooting a Slow or Missing Connection

- **Restart your router.** Unplug it from the wall, wait 30 seconds, then plug it back in. This fixes most problems.
- **Move closer to the router.** Walls and distance weaken the signal.
- **Restart your device.** Turn it off and back on again.
- **Check for outages.** Call your internet provider if nothing works — there may be an outage in your area.

## Speed Test Basics

Wondering if your internet is running slowly? Visit **speedtest.net** in your web browser and tap **Go**. It will show your download speed. For most home use, anything above 25 Mbps (megabits per second) is fine for video calls and streaming.

## Wi-Fi Security

Your home Wi-Fi should always be protected with a password. Modern routers use a security type called **WPA3** (or WPA2), which scrambles your data so strangers cannot read it. Never share your Wi-Fi password with people you do not trust.

## Public Wi-Fi Safety

Coffee shops, libraries, and airports often offer free Wi-Fi. Be careful on these networks:

- **Do not** log into your bank or enter credit card numbers on public Wi-Fi.
- **Do not** shop online on public Wi-Fi.
- If you must use public Wi-Fi, avoid anything involving passwords or personal information.
