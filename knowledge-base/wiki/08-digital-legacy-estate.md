## Guide 1: How to Create a Digital Estate Plan

**Slug:** `how-to-create-digital-estate-plan`
**Category:** `safety-guides`
**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Read Time:** 8 min
**Tags:** digital-estate, passwords, accounts, legacy, family, planning
**Emoji:** 📋

### Excerpt
Make sure your family can access your important digital accounts and files if something happens to you — a digital estate plan keeps everything organized.

### Steps

1. **Understand what a digital estate is**
   - Your digital estate includes every online account you own: email, banking, social media, subscriptions, photo storage, and more. Without a plan, your family may not be able to access important accounts.

2. **Make a list of your accounts**
   - Write down every online account you have. Include the website, your username or email, and what the account is for. Common ones: email (Gmail, Yahoo, Outlook), banking, Social Security (ssa.gov), insurance portals, Facebook, Amazon, Netflix, utility accounts.
   - *Tip:* Check your email inbox for "Welcome" or "Account Created" messages to jog your memory about accounts you may have forgotten.

3. **Store your passwords securely**
   - Use a password manager (like 1Password or Bitwarden) and share the master password with a trusted family member. Alternatively, write passwords on paper and store them in a fireproof safe or with your attorney.
   - *Warning:* Do not store passwords in a regular document on your computer or in an unsecured note on your phone.

4. **Set up legacy contacts and inactive account managers**
   - **Apple:** Go to Settings → [Your Name] → Sign-In & Security → Legacy Contact. Add someone who can access your Apple account after your death.
   - **Google:** Go to myaccount.google.com → Data & Privacy → Make a plan for your digital legacy. Set an "Inactive Account Manager" who can access your data.
   - **Facebook:** Go to Settings → Memorialization Settings. Choose a "Legacy Contact" who can manage your profile.

5. **Document your wishes**
   - Write a brief letter explaining what you want done with each account: keep it active, download photos, close it, etc. Include this with your will or estate documents.

6. **Tell your trusted person**
   - Choose one or two people you trust completely. Tell them where to find your account list and passwords. Make sure they know your wishes.
   - *Tip:* Review and update this plan once a year — accounts change, passwords get updated, and new accounts are created.

7. **Consider legal protections**
   - Some states have laws (the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act) that give executors the right to access digital accounts. Talk to your attorney about including digital assets in your will.

## Guide 2: How to Share Passwords Safely with Family

**Slug:** `how-to-share-passwords-with-family`
**Category:** `safety-guides`
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Read Time:** 5 min
**Tags:** passwords, family, sharing, security, emergency
**Emoji:** 👨‍👩‍👧

### Excerpt
Share important passwords with a trusted family member so they can help you in an emergency — without putting your security at risk.

### Steps

1. **Choose who to trust**
   - Pick one or two people — typically a spouse, adult child, or sibling — who you trust with your most important accounts (email, banking, medical portals).

2. **Use a password manager with sharing**
   - Password managers like 1Password and Bitwarden let you share specific passwords with family members through a "shared vault." Each person has their own login, and you control what they can see.
   - *Tip:* The family plan for 1Password costs about $5/month and covers up to 5 people. Bitwarden's family plan is $3.33/month.

3. **Use Apple's shared passwords feature**
   - If your family uses iPhones, go to Settings → Passwords → Shared Passwords Group. Create a group and invite family members. Shared passwords sync securely across all Apple devices.

4. **Write it down as a backup**
   - Write your most critical passwords (email, banking, medical) on paper. Store the paper in a sealed envelope in a fireproof safe or a bank safe deposit box.
   - *Warning:* Never email, text, or post passwords. These methods are not secure and can be intercepted.

5. **Use an "emergency sheet"**
   - Create one page with these essentials: main email and password, bank login, phone unlock code, Apple ID or Google account password, and the name and password for your password manager.

6. **Update regularly**
   - When you change a password, update the shared vault or emergency sheet. Set a calendar reminder to review every 6 months.

### Official Sources

- [Bitwarden Families](https://bitwarden.com/pricing/)

## Guide 3: How to Set Up Google Inactive Account Manager

**Slug:** `how-to-set-up-google-inactive-account-manager`
**Category:** `safety-guides`
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Read Time:** 5 min
**Tags:** google, legacy, inactive-account, digital-estate, data
**Emoji:** 🔐

### Excerpt
Tell Google what to do with your account if you stop using it — share your data with trusted contacts or have it deleted automatically.

### Steps

1. **Go to the Inactive Account Manager page**
   - Sign in to your Google account. Go to myaccount.google.com/inactive.

2. **Set a wait time**
   - Choose how long Google should wait after you stop signing in before considering your account inactive. Options are 3, 6, 12, or 18 months. Google will try to contact you before taking any action.

3. **Add trusted contacts**
   - Add the email addresses of people you want to receive your data. For each person, you can choose which data they receive: Gmail, Google Photos, Google Drive, YouTube, and more.
   - *Tip:* Add at least one family member and consider adding a second as a backup.

4. **Choose whether to delete your account**
   - You can optionally tell Google to delete your entire account 3 months after sharing your data. If you skip this, your account stays as-is.

5. **Add a personal message (optional)**
   - Write a message that will be sent to your trusted contacts when the plan activates. Something like "Please download my photos and share them with the family."

6. **Review and confirm**
   - Read through your plan. Click "Review Plan" then "Confirm." You can change these settings anytime.
   - *Tip:* Google will send you periodic reminders to make sure your plan is still up to date.

### Official Sources
- [Google Inactive Account Manager](https://myaccount.google.com/inactive)
- [Google Support: About Inactive Account Manager](https://support.google.com/accounts/answer/3036546)

## Guide 4: How to Memorialize or Delete a Loved One's Facebook Account

**Slug:** `how-to-memorialize-facebook-account`
**Category:** `safety-guides`
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Read Time:** 4 min
**Tags:** facebook, memorial, death, social-media, family, legacy
**Emoji:** 🕯️

### Excerpt
When a loved one passes away, you can memorialize their Facebook profile as a tribute or request its permanent removal.

### Steps

1. **Understand your options**
   - **Memorialize:** The profile stays up with "Remembering" next to the name. Friends can share memories on the timeline. No one can log in to the account.
   - **Remove:** The profile and all its content are permanently deleted.

2. **Request memorialization**
   - Go to facebook.com/help/contact/305593649477238. Fill out the form with the person's name, their profile URL, and proof of death (obituary link or death certificate upload).

3. **Request removal**
   - Go to facebook.com/help/contact/228813257197480. You will need to prove you are an immediate family member and provide proof of death.

4. **If they had a Legacy Contact**
   - If the person had set up a Legacy Contact before they passed, that person can manage the memorialized profile — add a pinned post, respond to friend requests, and update the profile photo.

5. **Protect against scams**
   - If someone contacts you claiming they can "unlock" a deceased person's account for a fee, it is a scam. Only Facebook can manage memorialized accounts.
   - *Warning:* Never give anyone money or personal information to access a deceased person's social media.

### Official Sources
- [Facebook: Memorialized Accounts](https://www.facebook.com/help/1568013990080948)
- [Facebook: Request Removal](https://www.facebook.com/help/contact/228813257197480)
